Law Of Team Work

  1. Law of Significance: One is too small a number to achieve greatness.

    2. Maxwell asserts that nothing of significance was ever achieved by an individual acting alone. A Chinese Proverb agrees, “Behind an able man, there are always other able men.” And Andrew Carnegie pointed out, “It marks a big step in your development when you come to realize that other people can help you do a better job than you could do alone.”

  2. Law of the Big Picture: The goal is more important than the role.

    3. The 1950’s Book of Football Wisdom stated, “If a team is to reach its full potential, each player must be willing to subordinate his personal goals to the good of the team.” Ray Kroc, Founder of McDonald’s, had a similar philosophy: “No one of us is more important than the rest of us.”

  3. Law of the Niche: All players have a place where they add the most value.

    4. Leaders who want to help people reach their potential and maximize their effectiveness should stretch team members out of their comfort zones but never out of their gift zones.

  4. Law of Mt. Everest: As the challenge escalates, the need for teamwork elevates.

    5. Tenzig Norway, a 1930’s mountain climber, said, “You do not climb a mountain like Everest by trying to race ahead on your own or competing with your comrades.” Maxwell points out that one mistake people make is they focus too much on their dream and too little on their team. If you build the right team, the dream will almost take care of itself.

  5. Law of the Chain: Strength of the team is impacted by its weakest link.

    6. Maxwell notes that one of the mistakes he made early in his career as a leader was that he thought everyone who was on his team should remain on the team. He would later learn that for weak links, you need to train or trade them. Somebody who is a weak link on your team might be capable of becoming a star on another team.

    If you need to remove somebody from the team, be discreet, clear, honest, and brief. Then, once the person is gone, be open about it with the rest of the team while maintaining respect for the person you let go.

  6. Law of the Catalyst: Winning Teams have players who make things happen.

    7. This law states that in a group setting, such as a sports team or work team, the most successful teams always have certain key players who function as "catalysts." They sparks action and drive results to help the team win. A few catalysts end up propelling the whole team forward much faster through this catalytic effect than just having a few hard workers.

  7. Law of the Compass: Vision gives team members direction and confidence.

    8. A compelling vision builds on the past (it doesn’t diminish it). Jack Welch of GE argued, “Without question, communicating the vision, and the atmosphere around the vision, has been, and is continuing to be, by far, the toughest job we face.”

  8. Law of the Bad Apple: Rotten attitudes ruin a team.

    9. This law focuses on the very detrimental effects that just one negative individual can have on a team environment. Like "a bad apple that spoils the bunch", such toxic team members spread negativity, resentment, and pessimism that brings down morale, cooperation, and progress.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Examples of "Bad Apples": The chronic complainer, overly critical micromanager, unmotivated slacker, defiant pessimist. Regardless of nominal talent, they corrode team cohesion.

    • Negative Ripple Effects: Teammates absorb bad attitudes through close contact. Soon complaining replaces creating. Critiquing outweighs collaborating. Vibes shift from optimistic to uneasy. Progress slows.

    • Rationalizations Backfire: Attempts to neutralize bad apples fail. Manager interventions falter ("They bring unique skills when not grumbling"). Rottenness seems to indoctrinate others faster than positives uplift them.

    • Solution is Removal: Wise leaders realize revived team spirit, trust and productivity necessitate cutting out the decay. A bold "bad apple ejection" cleanses the bunch.

    In short, just like toxins infecting a physical body, one or two negative spirited members often impair high performing teams from realizing their potential. The cure is decisive removal rather than flawed tolerance.

  9. Law of Countability: Teammates must be able to count on each other when it counts

    10. This law emphasizes the importance of reliability amongst teammates. In high stakes situations, whether an important project deadline or critical moment in a game, great teams have members that their peers know they can absolutely count on to deliver when it truly matters most.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Defining Countability: It refers to one's track record of stepping up, following through and performing in the clutch on responsibilities big and small. Can teammates bet their season, funding, or launch date on you without hesitation?

    • Why It's Crucial: Teams that lack countability point fingers in crunch time while those with high accountability thrive. The security of being able to count on others' best efforts reduces collective anxiety during tense times.

    • Assessing & Improving Countability: Managers can examine past tendencies but also improve it on teams by equally distributing duties, cross-training as backup, and framing projects as all-for-one efforts.

    • Rewarding Rockstars: Publicly praise and reward those with exceptional reliability records. Their positive countability influences and motivates others to mirror such steadfastness.

    In closing, in times where it feels like the whole team result hinges on an individual's output, confidence in a peer's countability becomes a tremendous competitive advantage. Savvy leaders instill this quality deliberately.

  10. Law of the Price Tag: Team fails to reach its potential when it fails to pay the price.

    11. This law centers around the direct relationship between a team's willingness to "pay the price" through hard work, sacrifice and resilience - and reaching its true peak performance. When urgency and commitment to doing what it takes is lacking, potential sadly never shifts to results.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • The Price Tag Metaphor: Championship teams view success like a item with a clear price tag. They eagerly count costs then willingly invest sweat and dedication to afford it. Underperforming teams hope for discounts and sales.

    • Assessing Willingness: Managers can gauge a team's drive to pay the price by auditing work capacity, accountability, grit, collective buy-in and sacrifice tolerance. What are they willing to bleed to achieve goals?

    • Instilling an Owner Mentality: Reframing team members as active owners rather than mere participants greatly increases their motivation to do whatever it takes to see high returns. Owners treat investment differently.

    • Celebrating Price Paying: Leaders should praise examples of paying the full price, from late night work sessions to voluntary training. This cultures an environment where no amount of cost feels too great.

    In closing, championship glory necessitates an abundant willingness to tirelessly invest hard work, time and resolve. By making checks out to the full price tag required, victories beautifully follow.

  11. Law of the Scoreboard: Team can make adjustments when it knows where it stands

    12. This law focuses on the importance of clear, visible measurement and immediate results/scoreboards for teams to gauge performance. Just like in sports, accurate real-time data showing current standing fuels both crucial adjustments and increased motivation.

    Expanded Explanation
    • Types of Scoreboards KPI dashboards, rankings, progress reports, budget metrics. Clear means for quantifying work gives clarity.

    • Driving Adjustments If lagging in a particular area, Identifying it quickly allows for adapted strategy before major consequence.

    • Motivation Boosts Seeing in real-time when work positively impacts numbers incentivizes increased effort. Transparency reveals causation.

    • Pitfalls of Estimating No scoreboard yields guesswork. Teams left estimating how results are coming risk drifting complacent until year-end report cards.

    In summary, scoreboards afford teams the same dynamic feedback loops leveraged by champion sports organizations. By frequently checking where they stand, hungry teams make moves that ultimately put numbers in their favor.

  12. Law of the Bench: Great teams have great depth.

    13. This law highlights how in team sports, championships are overwhelmingly won by teams with top-tier talent not just on the starting roster but also on the bench. The backups possess capabilities close to starters. This depth fortifies them against injuries, fatigue, and gameplay uncertainties.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Definition of "Bench": A team's subs, reserves, replacements. Non-starters waiting in the wings to tap in. Championship benches boast starting-caliber players, while struggling teams have major drop-offs in ability.

    • Importance of Depth: Seasons and games have ups and downs. Illness, injuries, fatigue are inevitable. Having reserves as able as starters prepares a team to minimally lose momentum when unforeseen situations require substituting.

    • Scouting & Development: Savvy teams pipeline capable talent through meticulous scouting and player development programs. Investing in the bench pays returns.

    In summary, championship teams demonstrate greatness top to bottom on rosters to ready them for the war of attrition that is a long season. Carefully crafted depth acts as insurance when the going gets tough.

  13. Law of Identity: Shared values define the team.

    14. This law states that teams with the strongest sense of identity are those that rally around core values shared amongst members. Defining the collective's principles and purpose - what they stand for - is what binds them together during adversity and spotlights direction when facing decisions.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Types of Shared Values: Trust, innovation, excellence, grit, selflessness, and inclusion are example values championship teams often unite around.

    • Benefits of Shared Identity: Camaraderie and chemistry heighten. Members buy into team-first mentality. Confidence emerges in who they are. Values drive goals and governance.

    • Crafting Team Identity: Leaders must facilitate discovering, articulating and embedding shared values. What convictions or standards does the group want associated to its signature culture?

    • Reinforcing the Identity: Reference the principles during trying times to refocus collective purpose. Celebrate displays of the values publicly. Show prospects how the identity aligns to their character.

    In closing, at the heart of teams who achieve sustained greatness lies a value set that centers and defines them. Their shared identity guides behavior and unlocks conviction in who they are together.

  14. Law of Communication: Interaction fuels action.

    15. This law spotlights the importance of ongoing communication in enabling team execution. The level of direct contact between members correlates to coordination, collaboration and accountability for results. Constant interaction fuels aligned progress.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Forms of Communication: Meetings, huddles, messaging channels, knowledge bases and informal conversations all facilitate the exchange of insights and information amongst team members.

    • Why It Matters: Frequent communication circumvents roadblocks before they derail efforts, surfaces solutions, and keeps productivity humming through relationship building and idea transfers.

    • Symptoms of Poor Communication: Missed deliverable deadlines, duplicated work, limited cooperation, feeling out of loop, stalled decisions, lack of awareness around issues arising.

    • Investing in Rich Communication: Savvy leaders structure plenty of face-to-face interaction via stand ups, workshops, showcases, social events rather than just emails. The chemistry fuels excellence.

    In summary, communication acts as the lifeblood through which the components of a coordinated team stay nourished, informed, connected, and progressing. Prioritizing open channels pays dividends.

  15. Law of the Edge: Difference between two equally talented teams is leadership.

    16. This law centers on how in sports, business, and every other team context, a marginal performance edge over time emerges from leadership strength as the primary differentiating variable when comparing groups of equal talent. Better coaching and ability to get the most out of the components predicts winners.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • Leadership = influence capable of driving team outcomes Through vision, strategy, motivation, accountability, development

    • Examples of Leadership Impact: Two sales teams with similar talent - the one structured with stronger coaching and team building outperforms the other over the fiscal year.

    • Requirement of Equal Talent: This law assumes similar player personnel/staff capabilities to control for variables. Only proves true if personnel aptitude held constant.

    • Increased Necessity in Adversity: In high pressure crucible moments of uncertainty, volatility or complexity, quality of leadership becomes an even stronger predictor of which teams crack or thrive. Calm guidance separates.

    In closing, when teams possessing analogous skill levels and tools compete directly, superior orchestrating of the parts through adroit leadership repeatedly provides the slim dividing line between greatness and mediocrity over time.

  16. Law of High Morale: When you’re winning, nothing hurts.

    17. This law focuses on the peak sense of optimism, energy and even invincibility teams experience when riding the momentum of consistent victories. High morale resulting from sustained periods of winning masks typical pains, obstacles seem conquerable and confidence soars collectively.

    Expanded Explanation:
    • The Snowball Effect: Early wins and achievements lead to raised expectations, self-assurance, swagger. Soon teams attack all obstacles aggressively, further fueled by succeeding. A positive feedback loop forms raising overall morale sky-high.

    • Signs of High Morale: Laughing at grind, visualizing bigger goals, increased risk-taking and loyalty between members. Teams in rhythm weather storms smiling.

    • Leveraging Peak Morale Windows: Wise leaders recognize optimal morale spikes as green lights for pivotal action items requiring courage, sacrifice or change otherwise hard to orchestrate. Possibilities seem within reach.

    In closing, teams lucky enough to catch fire through stacks of wins discover that a magical type of collective morale emerges making the journey ahead glide instead of grind. But it's usually short-lived peaks requiring prompt capitalization.

  17. Law of Dividends: Investing in the team compounds over time

    18. This law centers on the exponential returns teams enjoy when they deliberately invest in their culture, capabilities, and infrastructure for the long-term rather than maintaining only a short-term transactional mindset. Like financial dividends, the effects compound.

    Expanded Analysis:
    • Areas for Investment: Team building, professional development, streamlined processes, updated performance tools, social events, recognition programs.

    • Compounding Effect Over Time: Trust, execution skills, information sharing, job satisfaction and cohesion gradually amplify across a team the longer investments sustain. Momentum strengthens results.

    • Pitfalls of Underinvesting: Stagnating growth, lack of innovation, culture erosion, turnover risk. Failing to enrich a team's soil leads to diminishing returns.

    • Sustaining Dividends Leaders should track health metrics and consistently reinvest a portion of returns. Seeking quick profits at the expense of nurturing the golden goose spells evential doom.

    In closing, an owner mentality focused on stewarding resources for lasting maximized yield puts teams in optimal positions to realize aggregating returns as compound interest kicks in. But it requires patience and care.
